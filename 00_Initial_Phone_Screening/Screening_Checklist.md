# 🗓️ Thursday Initial Phone Screening: Extra Preparation notes

This document provides additional strategies and quick tips specifically for your screening call this Thursday. It covers tricky questions, logistics, and how to present yourself as a strong Google candidate.

---

## 🚨 Golden Rules for the Recruiter Call
1. **Be Concise:** Recruiters speak to many candidates daily. Keep your answers under 2 minutes. Focus on **What you did** and **What the impact was**.
2. **Be Honest but Strategic:** Don't oversell something you know zero about. If asked about a skill you don't know, say: *"I haven't strictly worked on that, but I have worked on [similar technology] and I am a quick learner."*
3. **Sound Enthusiastic:** Google values engineers who are genuinely passionate about technology and large-scale problems.

---

## 💼 Handling Tricky Logistics Questions

### 1. "What is your Notice Period?"
*Why it matters:* Recruiters get worried about 90-day notice periods (which is common in service-based companies like TCS).
**If you have 90 days, say:** 
> "My official notice period is 90 days, but I have accrued unused vacation days, and I can negotiate it down to 60 days (or even 45 days) with my manager once an offer is finalized."

### 2. "What are your salary expectations?"
*Golden Rule:* Try NOT to give a specific number right away. You lose negotiation power.
**What to say:** 
> "Right now, my primary focus is finding the right role where I can contribute to large-scale systems and grow as an engineer. I am very confident that Google offers highly competitive compensation, and I am open to standard market ranges for this level."
*(If the recruiter absolutely forces you to give a number, state your current CTC and mentioned you expect a "standard competitive hike based on market rates").*

## 💡 Quick-Fire Technical Trivia
Sometimes Google recruiters have a spreadsheet of 3-5 basic technical questions. They aren't deeply technical themselves, they just read the question and check if your answer matches their buzzwords. Read these quick answers:

*   **Q: What is the main advantage of microservices over a monolith?**
    > **A:** "Independent deployment and horizontal scalability. If one specific service fails, the entire application doesn't crash."
*   **Q: Why do you actively use Redis?**
    > **A:** "Redis stores data in memory (RAM) instead of on a disk, making data retrieval extremely fast and reducing the load on the main database."
*   **Q: What is the primary difference between React and Angular?**
    > **A:** "React is primarily a UI library focused on components and uses a Virtual DOM. Angular is a full-fledged robust framework with built-in utilities like routing, HTTP clients, and dependency injection."
*   **Q: What is a REST API?**
    > **A:** "It's an architectural style for an API that uses standard HTTP methods like GET, POST, PUT, and DELETE to communicate and transfer data between client and server ideally in JSON format."

## 📈 Emphasizing Your "Impact" (Google loves this!)
Google recruiters are trained to look for measurable impact. Whenever you can, add a "metric" to your sentences:
*   *Instead of:* "I migrated the application to Spring Boot."
    👉 *Say:* "I migrated the legacy app to Spring Boot microservices, which **reduced our deployment time by half** and significantly **lowered system latency**."
*   *Instead of:* "I moved our app to ACA."
    👉 *Say:* "By moving to Azure Container Apps, we implemented auto-scaling to zero, which **drastically reduced our monthly Azure billing costs**."
*   *Instead of:* "I built the UI."
    👉 *Say:* "I built the UI using lazy-loading, which **reduced our initial page load time** by seconds and improved user experience."

## 🧩 "Googliness" / Culture Fit Checks
Even in the first call, they check if you are a nice person who fits the culture.
*   **Teamwork:** If asked about your role, mention that as a Tech Lead, you actively collaborate with QA, DevOps, and Junior developers. "I believe in rising together and code reviews."
*   **Dealing with Ambiguity:** Google loves people who solve problems that aren't perfectly defined. Mention that you enjoy taking vague business requirements and turning them into stable architectures.
